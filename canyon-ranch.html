01 · The Story — Problem, vision & what we set out to do

40 years of paper booklets — and $300 walking out the door

Canyon Ranch relied entirely on analog scheduling. Paper booklets, front-desk phone calls, and Wellness Guides spending 20+ hours a week on admin tasks they were never hired to do. The business was losing revenue it couldn't see. Guests were leaving services on the table — not because they didn't want them, but because booking was friction.

"I was going to book a personal trainer, but the line at the desk was too long. I walked away, and they lost $300. If I could've just tapped it on an app, it would've been done."

— Guest interview
📋
Guests needed 6–7 iterations to finalize their schedule
No single view of the day. No self-service option. Scheduling meant paper booklets and multiple trips to the front desk — every time, every change.
⏱
Wellness Guides spent 20+ hrs/week on admin scheduling tasks
Guides hired to advise on health and wellness were consumed by routine booking. Little time remained for the high-value coaching Canyon Ranch marketed.
💸
Missed revenue — guests weren't discovering services easily
Poor discoverability meant guests didn't know what was available. If they couldn't find or book a service easily, Canyon Ranch lost the transaction with no visibility into how often it happened.
The Vision
How might we create a mobile app that enhances the customer experience & helps the business scale, by making booking the right services a breeze?
Goals: Increase NPS · Reduce Guide admin load · Increase on-ranch ancillary revenue
Goal 01
Empower guests with self-service booking
Give guests the ability to explore and book services independently, on their own device, without front-desk involvement.
Goal 02
Free Guides to become true advisors
Remove routine admin so Wellness Guides focus entirely on high-value coaching and complex guest wellness needs.
Goal 03
Drive higher NPS and on-ranch spend
Better discoverability + easier booking → more services booked → increased ancillary revenue and guest satisfaction.

03 · The Details — Hard decisions & things we had to get right

Three moments that could have broken the project

Each phase surfaced new complexity — API dependencies, guest anxiety, and a taxonomy breakdown that threatened adoption before launch. Here are the three decisions that mattered most, and exactly how I navigated each one.

Challenge 1Roadmap — US APIs weren't ready, but the India team needed work now
The Problem

Travis (US engineer) was still setting up APIs. The India dev team needed work immediately. Design was still iterating on UI/UX. Three streams had hard interdependencies with no clean sequencing.

What We Did

Tyler created APIs + mock data. India used mock data APIs + greyscale wireframes to build first. Hi-fi designs and real data were swapped in later. We called this the "wire-first, real-data-later" pattern.

The Outcome

All three teams stayed unblocked without slipping on key milestones — user testing, Beta plan — none of it moved. The pattern held clean through alpha launch.

My Role

I negotiated the build pattern. I was the connector — aligning research, design, and engineering timelines simultaneously, while negotiating tradeoffs to keep velocity high across US + India time zones.

Challenge 2Conflict Alerts — guests anxious about double-booking free and paid services
The Problem

Guests were concerned about double-booking. The original design didn't surface conflicts, leaving guests uncertain whether their schedule was valid — a real source of stress at the booking moment.

What We Did

Added conflict detection and inline alerts. Tested with 8 guests — 4 new, 4 returning — in one sprint before shipping. Before/after screenshots confirmed the change was understood immediately.

The Outcome

Guests described it as "must-have." Revised flow reduced frustration and boosted booking clarity. No negative feedback in any subsequent round of testing.

My Role

Scoped a lightweight but effective version with the designer — functional without derailing delivery velocity. Made the call that this was must-have, not nice-to-have, and held that position under schedule pressure.

"Conflicts is such a good feature." — P28, Tucson testing session
On-ranch prototype testing at the Tucson property — iterating and re-testing until we reached a solid direction
Challenge 3Taxonomy — 7 of 8 guests couldn't find services under existing categories
The Problem

When asked to find a massage, guests looked in Body Treatments, Medical Spa, Signature Services, and Health & Performance. Canyon Ranch's internal category structure didn't match guest mental models.

What We Did

Card sorting exercise: 16 participants, 4 moderated + 12 unmoderated. Produced a similarity matrix showing how guests naturally cluster services. Let the data redefine the IA, not the business org chart.

The Outcome

8/8 guests navigated to massages under the revised "Spa & Beauty" on the first attempt. App ease-of-use rating: 2.25/5 (1 = very easy) — consistent at both Tucson and Lenox.

My Role

Made the call to push for more testing even though it impacted timeline. Facilitated alignment between Design (wanted more time) and Engineering (wanted clarity). Kept leadership bought in on the tradeoff.

05 · Retrospect — What went well & what I'd do differently

The honest assessment of a 5-month 0-to-1 build

The things that went well were mostly about decisions made early. The things I'd do differently are about front-loading — getting ahead of problems before they cost time on a compressed timeline.

Post-launch reflection — Canyon Ranch, Tucson
What went well
Research-driven iteration throughoutTesting with real guests every week — not just at the start — kept every design decision grounded in actual behavior. The war-room debrief format after each testing round was particularly effective for capturing themes quickly.
Deep cross-functional alignment heldGetting all eight Canyon Ranch stakeholders to sign off on a shared MVP thesis in week two meant decisions stuck and didn't get re-litigated. That alignment held through launch.
Tangible impact on how people actually workThe product changed how Wellness Guides spent their days. When a product genuinely frees people from tasks they were never meant to do, it earns trust fast — and the 78% adoption reflected that.
What I'd do differently
Simplify taxonomy soonerThe card sorting exercise was the most valuable research we ran — but it happened mid-project. Running it in week one would have shaped the information architecture from the start and saved significant beta iteration time.
Onboard the India engineering team earlierThe mock data API solution worked, but the dependency lag created compressible risk. A shared integration timeline from week one would have helped both teams move faster in parallel.
Extend the app into post-visit engagementThe app was entirely on-ranch. Maintaining the wellness relationship after checkout — habit tracking, program reminders, pre-visit planning — was the obvious next frontier. We left it entirely untapped.
