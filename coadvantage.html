00 · Context — Who I am & where this sits

Principal PM owning product strategy for a platform scaling to 250K users

I'm Chris Pennell — a Product Manager and UX Lead with 10+ years building digital products across healthcare, wellness, and enterprise software, currently completing my MBA at Rice University. At the time of this project I was Principal PM at InfoBeans, a product development consultancy.

I owned product strategy and execution for CoAdvantage's platform redesign — defining the roadmap, prioritizing features, leading cross-functional delivery, and ensuring every design decision was grounded in real user research. The challenge: transform a feature-rich but friction-heavy HR platform without disrupting 105,000 active users.

My scope

I owned strategy through delivery. Roadmap definition, feature prioritization, cross-functional execution with engineering and design, user research, and stakeholder alignment — end to end in 3 months.

My north star: make the platform self-evident enough that a new user can complete their first HR task without calling support.

The business context

CoAdvantage is an HR and payroll platform serving small and mid-size businesses. With plans to scale from 105,000 to 250,000+ users, the existing UI and navigation patterns would actively work against growth.

A 3-month window to redesign, research-validate, and hand off to engineering — with no ability to break what was already working for 105K users.

01 · The Story — Problem & what we set out to do

Feature-rich but friction-heavy — a platform holding itself back

Despite offering a comprehensive HR platform, CoAdvantage Quantum was constrained by an outdated UI and confusing user flows. With plans to grow from 105,000 to 250,000+ users, they needed a complete UX transformation to remain competitive.

🖥
Outdated interface lacked modern aesthetics and responsiveness
The UI hadn't kept pace with user expectations or competitive alternatives in the HR tech market.
🔀
Inefficient navigation led to frustration and increased support needs
Users couldn't find key functions without guidance. Every navigation failure became a support call.
📈
Platform needed to support 2–3× growth without breaking core workflows
Scaling without architectural and UX work would amplify every existing problem across a larger user base.
🏁
Onboarding and task flows required more autonomy for end-users
Self-service gaps meant new users were dependent on support during the most critical adoption window.
02 · Customer & Data Insights — Research, strategy & execution

Strategic planning → customer-centric design → validated delivery

I collaborated closely with executive stakeholders, engineering, design, and customer support to identify key business goals and technical constraints. Together we mapped strategic use cases that would drive both UX improvements and growth enablement.

Phase 1
Strategic Planning & Execution Oversight
Define and socialize product vision, prioritize initiatives, drive roadmap
What I did
  • Defined and socialized the product vision and roadmap with executives and department leads
  • Prioritized initiatives based on business impact, user value, and technical feasibility
  • Drove feature scoping, sprint planning, backlog grooming, and delivery milestones
Prioritization framework
  • Business impact: which improvements directly support the 250K+ growth target?
  • User value: which friction points generate the most support volume?
  • Technical feasibility: what can be delivered in 3 months vs. backlogged?
  • Competitive parity: what does the market now expect as table-stakes UX?
Phase 2
Customer-Centric Design Leadership
Reimagine the user experience, deliver design system, reduce onboarding friction
What shipped
  • Customizable homepage dashboard improving daily engagement and task visibility
  • Overhauled HR workflow templates reducing onboarding time and user drop-off
  • Scalable design system enabling consistent UI/UX patterns across the platform
How I led it
  • Led the product definition process with an associate designer to reimagine the UX
  • Partnered with engineering to define and implement the design system architecture
  • Ensured every design decision connected back to a validated user need or business goal
Redesigned dashboard and workflow templates — improved discoverability and task completion
Phase 3
User Research & Validation
Validate designs with real users, apply feedback iteratively before dev investment
What I did
  • Conducted in-depth user interviews to uncover key pain points across user personas
  • Led high-fidelity prototyping and iterative usability testing
  • Applied structured feedback to continuously refine key flows pre-development
What we validated
  • Dashboard customization significantly improved perceived control and daily return rate
  • Revised workflow templates reduced time-to-completion on core HR tasks
  • Design system patterns were consistently understood across different user personas

04 · Retrospect — What went well & what I'd do differently

What a 3-month engagement taught me about speed and scope

What went well
Executive alignment enabled fast decisionsHaving direct access to executive stakeholders from day one meant prioritization decisions were made quickly and stuck.
Design system unlocked velocityInvesting early in a shared design system meant the second half of the project moved significantly faster than the first.
Customer support as a research channelSupport team insights — especially ticket patterns — proved to be one of the richest sources of user pain data available.
What I'd do differently
Run usability testing earlier in the cycleWith a 3-month timeline, testing happened later than ideal. Earlier testing would have freed up the back half of the project for more iteration.
Map the full user journey before scopingA complete journey map upfront would have surfaced some of the workflow dependencies we discovered mid-project earlier in the process.
Extend the engagement into post-launch monitoringWithout a post-launch phase, validating the impact of the redesign required the client team to set up their own measurement — something I should have built into the plan.
