Turning a messy provider search into a product teams could actually work through

Trmeric was trying to help companies define technology work, find the right providers, track delivery, and manage transactions in one place. I was brought in to turn that broad idea into clearer flows, stronger UX structure, and a prototype direction the team could build from.

Project goals

The work centered on three things: help buyers define what they needed before they chose a provider, make the product easier to navigate across discovery and execution, and create a clearer operating layer for tracking providers, offers, and transactions.

This was not just screen polish. The product needed stronger workflow logic, a usable project brief, clearer status handling, and a home for the work once a customer moved beyond the first conversation.

00 · Context and Background

The idea was ambitious. The hard part was making the path through it feel clear.

Trmeric was trying to cover a lot of ground: help a company clarify its needs, generate a project brief, discover providers, manage offers, oversee delivery, and handle transactions. The opportunity was strong, but so was the risk. Without a clear structure, the product could easily become a stack of features instead of a usable operating system for the work.

The journey map, user stories, and working-session recordings all pointed at the same problem. Buyers often did not know the right way to kick off the work, providers were not always aligned with the actual need, and both sides were left filling in gaps the product should have helped close.

What the product needed

Better structured discovery. Users needed help turning a broad problem into a usable project brief and clearer provider criteria.

A stronger working surface. Once discovery was done, customers needed a clearer place to manage providers, offers, projects, and transactions.

Why it was tricky

The guesswork was on both sides. Customers were often unsure how to frame the work, and providers were often selling into incomplete requirements.

The platform had to do more than matchmake. It also had to support progress, status, collaboration, and decision-making after the first conversation.

01 · My Role and Scope

I was there to shape the product, not just decorate it.

The initial scope was organized as a four-sprint prototype plan. Week one covered Discover and Engage. Week two moved into Build and Retro. Week three focused on Transact and Action Hub. Week four pulled all six modules plus the home screen into one higher-fidelity prototype. Later retainer work extended into provider pages, second-level home-page definition, feature improvements from the working inventory, and a more reusable style and component system.

02 · Research and Discovery

The clearest signal was simple. People needed the product to remove uncertainty, not add to it.

The recordings and journey map make the same point in different ways. Buyers did not always know how to define the work, how to evaluate providers, or how to tell whether a proposal really fit the problem. The product had to reduce that uncertainty with structure, context, and clearer prompts.

“For the guy who's using this, consuming this is very difficult to know what's the right way.”
Working session, June 12, 2023
Priority 1: turn discovery into guided framing
The Discover flow had to help users define requirements, success metrics, constraints, timelines, and provider criteria instead of dropping them into a blank search experience.
Priority 2: make progress and context visible
Users needed to know where they were in the flow, how many steps remained, and what the system was helping them produce along the way.
Priority 3: make expertise feel real
The team kept coming back to the idea that the product could not feel generic. It had to show that it understood the customer's world and could help shape a better path through it.
Priority 4: create a more usable post-discovery home
Once users moved past discovery, they needed a better place to manage provider performance, offers, project health, transactions, and activity history.
03 · Key Product Decisions

A few decisions carried most of the weight. Each one made the system easier to understand and easier to use.

A later IA iteration from the `Initial Versions` PDF. It shows the product getting clearer about the main stages: demand definition, discovery, screening, contracting, work and delivery, payments, and post-delivery.
Provider Space home view. The product started to look more useful once the work had a clearer operating surface for status, provider comparison, and action-taking.
Action Hub customization screen. This is one of the missing pieces the archive makes clear: the product was not only about choosing a provider. It was also trying to give teams a configurable working home for projects, updates, and next actions once work was underway.
Decision 1
Make Discover a guided conversation that produces a real brief
Problem

Users were being asked to choose providers before the work was well defined, which made both search and evaluation weaker.

What I pushed toward

A structured discovery flow with contextual prompts, progress cues, upload support, and a generated project brief users could review, edit, and share.

Why it mattered

That shifted the product from “search for vendors” toward “help me define the work well enough to choose the right provider.”

Evidence: the Discover user stories explicitly define the conversational flow, progress bar, project brief generation, editing, and collaboration.
Decision 2
Give the work a clearer operating home after discovery
Problem

Matching users with providers was only part of the job. The product also needed a place to manage what happened after that decision.

What I pushed toward

Provider Space and Action Hub concepts that made room for summaries, project status, provider health, actions, and follow-on decisions.

Why it mattered

Without that layer, the platform would feel useful in the first conversation and thin everywhere after.

Evidence: the 2024 retainer expands into provider pages, home page structure, and reusable UI work, which reinforces that this became a core product layer.
Decision 3
Treat transact as a workflow, not just a list of payments
Problem

Transactions had different states, project types, and approval paths that could get confusing quickly if they were collapsed into a flat table.

What I pushed toward

A structure that showed workflow status, line-item detail, action buttons, dashboards, and different summary logic for time-and-materials versus fixed-price work.

Why it mattered

The payment layer needed to support trust and oversight, not just record that an invoice existed.

Evidence: the Transact story sheet lays out explicit state models, summary views, detail views, approvals, payments, notifications, and exports.
04 · Delivery Pattern

The work moved in stages. Start with the journeys, then make the interface earn its way into the story.

Another useful architecture artifact from the same PDF. It shows how the team was thinking about the system as more than a search tool: entities, contracting, feedback, a knowledge hub, and integrations were all part of the long-term shape.
Phase 1
Define the product path
Break the platform into priority flows and figure out what each module had to do before making the screens more detailed
What the work needed
  • Clarify Discover, Engage, Build, Retro, Transact, and Action Hub as distinct parts of the product.
  • Use interviews, journey mapping, and user stories to define what mattered most in each area.
  • Move through the work in a concrete sprint sequence instead of treating the prototype like one undifferentiated design pass.
How it played out
  • The team kept returning to stakeholder interviews, provider journeys, and backlog shaping as part of the design work.
  • There was explicit concern about trust, substance, and making the product feel tailored to the client's world.
  • The sprint plan itself gave the project structure: Discover and Engage first, Build and Retro next, then Transact and Action Hub, then one combined prototype.
Phase 2
Turn product structure into screens people could navigate
Translate the module logic into more resolved interface patterns for provider comparison, offers, action management, and transaction review
What mattered most
  • Make the system easier to scan with summaries, statuses, and action areas that felt useful at a glance.
  • Balance conversational discovery with more structured management views once work moved forward.
  • Keep the screens simple enough to use without flattening the real operational detail.
What the screens show
  • Provider Space gives the product a stronger monitoring and comparison layer.
  • Offer Space makes the marketplace side more tangible and easier to act on.
  • Action Hub pushes the product closer to an actual working home instead of a one-time matching tool.
  • The later retainer scope shows the system continuing into provider pages, home-page refinement, and component reuse.
Offer Space helped make the marketplace layer more concrete by showing curated offers, industry-relevant options, and a clearer path from interest to action.

05 · Outcomes and Strategic Value

The strongest outcome was direction. The product became much easier to picture as a real working system.

I do not have a clean business KPI to claim here, and I do not want to fake one. What the project materials do support is a stronger product structure: a better defined discovery flow, a clearer provider-management layer, more explicit transact logic, and a more reusable interface foundation for what came next.

Project takeaway
06 · Retrospect

What this project reinforced for me. A product like this lives or dies by how well it reduces ambiguity.

What went well
Started with the real uncertainty in the work The strongest part of the project was that it took buyer confusion, provider mismatch, and weak project framing seriously instead of treating them as side issues.
Tied screens back to workflow logic The UI work is strongest where it clearly supports a more useful operating model around discovery, provider management, and transact.
Left behind usable product artifacts The combination of user stories, journey map, screen files, and later retainer scope makes it possible to understand both the strategy and the interface direction.
What I'd do differently
Preserve more explicit workflow visuals The logic is well documented in sheets and recordings, but a tighter set of saved flow diagrams would make the story easier to tell quickly.
Keep the home-screen story tighter earlier The later recordings show that Action Hub and home-screen logic were still open questions. I would want that layer locked sooner.
Make collaboration artifacts easier to scan later There is a lot of valuable material in the archive, but some of the insight is spread across docs, sheets, and recordings instead of living in one clean decision trail.
What I would say about this work now

This project is strongest as an example of product strategy inside an emerging B2B platform. The job was to turn a promising but broad concept into a clearer product path with better framing, stronger module logic, and more useful screens.
