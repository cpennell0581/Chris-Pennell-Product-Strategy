00 · Context — Who I am & where this sits

Product Strategist & Fractional PO, shaping a 0-to-1 clinical trial platform

I'm Chris Pennell — a Product Manager and UX Lead with 10+ years building digital products across healthcare, wellness, and enterprise software, currently completing my MBA at Rice University. On this project I served as Product Strategist and Fractional Product Owner for Connect2Trials over an 8-month engagement.

I was responsible for shaping the MVP vision, aligning stakeholders across patients, clinical sites, CROs, and sponsors, and leading the platform through definition, design, and validation. My work laid the foundation for a scalable, human-centered clinical trial platform.

My scope

I owned MVP strategy through validated prototype. That meant: defining the product vision, aligning external clinical stakeholders, translating strategy into user stories and technical requirements, managing design execution, and running sprint planning.

My north star: build a platform patients actually trust enough to join a clinical trial through.

The domain context

Clinical trial recruitment has a well-documented engagement problem. Patients who qualify for trials often don't know they exist — and those who find one face an enrollment process designed for the research system, not for them.

Connect2Trials was built on the premise that better design can improve recruitment, engagement, and retention simultaneously. I was there to prove that out through an MVP.

01 · The Story — The opportunity & what we set out to do

Clinical trial recruitment is broken — patients pay the price

Connect2Trials is a web-based SaaS platform designed to support patients throughout their clinical trial journey. The company aimed to improve recruitment, engagement, and retention by making trials more accessible and emotionally resonant for patients.

The problem wasn't a lack of trials — it was a lack of patient-centered design. Enrollment processes were complex, intimidating, and built for the systems of clinical research rather than the humans navigating them.

🔍
Patients couldn't find or understand trials relevant to them
Discovery was opaque. Medical language and eligibility criteria created barriers that patients couldn't navigate independently.
🤝
Engagement dropped off once patients entered the funnel
Without trust-building mechanisms or ongoing support, patients disengaged before completing qualification or enrollment.
🔗
No platform addressed all three stakeholder groups cohesively
Sponsors, Sites, and CROs each had distinct workflows. Existing tools served one group at the expense of the others.
Patients
End users navigating clinical trial participation. Primary design priority — trust, literacy, and confidence were essential.
Sites & CROs
Clinical teams managing recruitment and trial execution. Needed tools that fit into existing workflows without adding friction.
Sponsors
Pharmaceutical and research sponsors funding and overseeing trials. Needed visibility into recruitment performance and outcomes.
02 · Customer & Data Insights — Human-centered research & strategy

Four sprint-based phases from MVP definition to validated prototype

I organized the work into four distinct phases, each spanning two-week sprints, aimed at specific objectives. The project's main priority was to address patient user flows for the MVP and conduct user testing. Patient experience was at the core — but the platform had to serve all three stakeholder groups without compromising any of them.

Phase 1
MVP Strategy & Roadmap Ownership
Define MVP vision, structure delivery, align internal and external stakeholders
What I did
  • Defined the MVP vision and structured delivery into three sprint-based phases
  • Prioritized core user flows based on business impact and clinical user needs
  • Facilitated alignment across internal teams and external clinical stakeholders
  • Translated high-level strategy into user stories, workflows, and technical requirements
MVP flows prioritized
  • Patient Onboarding — simplified registration and qualification to reduce friction and improve conversion
  • Engagement — health literacy tools and transparency in the matching process to build patient confidence
  • Community — trust-building structures to foster peer connections and long-term retention
User stories and requirements framework — mapping patient, site, and sponsor needs
Phase 2
UX Framework & Mid-Fidelity Wireframes
Define information architecture and interaction models for all three stakeholder groups
What I did
  • Led co-design sessions with patients and internal stakeholders to uncover key pain points
  • Developed experience maps, interaction frameworks, and user personas
  • Used qualitative insights to align platform tone, language, and interaction patterns with patient trust levels
Design principles applied
  • Health literacy — every piece of content written for a Grade 8 reading level
  • Trust signals — transparency about trial process, timeline, and patient rights throughout
  • Progressive disclosure — complex information revealed only when the patient is ready

04 · Retrospect — Key learnings & what I'd do differently

What this taught me about building for trust in healthcare

Learning 1
Human-centered research is essential in healthcare SaaS. It shaped both tone and functionality of the product. A technically correct solution that patients don't trust is not a solution.
Learning 2
A modular, phased MVP approach accelerated iteration. Breaking the MVP into three tightly scoped flows — rather than building everything at once — de-risked the build and enabled faster learning.
Learning 3
Clinical objectives and user emotion must be aligned. Success came from connecting what the research system needed with what patients actually felt during the process — not just function.
Learning 4
Aligning business outcomes with user behavior was critical. Features that looked good on a roadmap but didn't match patient mental models were cut early — and the product was better for it.
