01 · The Story — Problem, vision & what we set out to do

Patients were prescribed programs they never finished — and clinicians had no idea

Physical rehabilitation has a well-documented adherence crisis. Studies consistently show 50–65% of patients don't complete their home exercise programs. The reasons aren't complicated: the exercises are repetitive, there's no feedback, no progress visibility, and no consequence for quitting.

For clinicians, this was invisible. Patients would say they'd done their exercises. Therapists had no way to verify it — and outcomes reflected that gap.

Research methodology — mapping the behavioral and clinical gap between prescription and completion
Rehab was boring and patients tuned out
Zero feedback, zero progress visibility, zero sense of accomplishment. Session times of 1–2 minutes were common. The experience offered no reason to continue.
Therapists were flying blind on adherence
No data between appointments. Treatment decisions based on patient self-report, which research shows is wildly inaccurate.
The market had no scalable engagement solution
The rehab tech space was full of scheduling tools and exercise libraries. Nobody had solved engagement. That was the Blue Ocean — and our thesis from day one.

The problem wasn't that patients didn't want to get better. The problem was that the experience we gave them made quitting feel completely rational.

02 · Customer & Data Insights — What drove every product decision

What ethnographic research inside rehab centers taught us about behavior change

Before writing a single user story, I spent weeks inside rehabilitation centers — watching, listening, and asking questions. Not running formal interviews with clipboards. Actually sitting in sessions, observing how patients interacted with their programs and where the system broke down.

What I found reframed the entire product problem. Patients didn't quit because they were lazy. They quit because the experience was designed for compliance, not motivation.

Key insight #1
Real-time feedback was the missing variable. Patients who received any form of immediate feedback stayed engaged significantly longer. It didn't need to be complex — it needed to be immediate.
Key insight #2
Progress visibility drove return behavior. Patients who could see their improvement over time were dramatically more likely to come back. Seeing progress made the pain feel purposeful.
Key insight #3
Therapist trust was the adoption gateway. Patients adopted what their therapist recommended. Any strategy that didn't prioritize therapist buy-in first was building on sand.
Key insight #4
Gamification had to earn clinical credibility. The word "gamification" made clinicians nervous. Our design had to feel medically intentional, not toy-like. Game mechanics had to be invisible inside a clinical experience.

03 · The Details — Hard decisions & key tradeoffs

Three calls that defined the product — and the company

Building a 0-to-1 product means making decisions with incomplete information, competing stakeholder needs, and real timeline pressure. Here are the three that mattered most.

Decision 1Therapist dashboard first — or patient experience first?
The tension

Our instinct was to build the patient-facing experience first — that's where the engagement problem lived. But research kept pointing us back to therapists as the adoption gateway.

What I decided

Co-develop both in parallel, with the therapist dashboard treated as an equal MVP priority — not a phase 2 feature. Hard sell to engineering, but critical for go-to-market.

The outcome

The therapist dashboard became our most powerful sales tool. Skeptical therapists became advocates — and advocates became referrals.

The insight: In clinical markets, the prescriber is the product's first customer. Winning the patient without winning the therapist is a dead end.
Decision 2Deprioritizing features that patients loved but therapists couldn't validate
The tension

Early pilot patients loved social features — leaderboards, comparison against other patients. Engagement metrics were significantly higher. But clinical advisors flagged that comparing recovery rates was clinically inappropriate.

What I decided

Cut social comparison from MVP entirely. Redirected gamification to be self-referential — patients competed only against their own previous performance.

The outcome

Short-term, engagement metrics dipped slightly. Long-term, this unlocked hospital and senior care partnerships that would have been impossible with social comparison features.

The insight: In regulated markets, features that patients love but clinicians can't defend become liabilities at the enterprise sales stage.
Decision 3When to stop iterating and start scaling
The tension

After year two, we had strong pilot data but a product that still felt rough. The clinical team wanted to keep refining. Sales had three hospital networks ready to sign. Engineering was stretched.

What I decided

Pushed to scale. The data was sufficient, core flows were solid, and known limitations were cosmetic, not functional. We expanded and fixed in parallel.

The outcome

Landed all three hospital networks within the quarter. The rough edges were addressed within two sprints. Waiting would have lost the market window entirely.

The insight: "Good enough to ship" and "good enough to scale" are different thresholds. Knowing which one you're at requires honest calibration, not just gut feel.
04 · The Product — What shipped and how it performed

From prototype to standard of care

The core product used the patient's camera or sensor device to detect movement in real time, evaluate exercise form, and deliver immediate feedback through a gamified interface. Patients saw their score, their streak, their improvement curve. Therapists saw everything.

05 · Retrospect — What went well & what I'd do differently

Seven years of lessons in building for behavior change

What went well
Ethnographic research before any design workSpending weeks observing real patient behavior gave us insights structured research would have missed.
Treating the therapist as the first customerBuilding therapist tooling as an MVP priority shaped our go-to-market, enterprise sales motion, and clinical credibility.
Hypothesis-driven developmentWe never built features we couldn't test. Every decision was framed as an assumption to validate, not a requirement to deliver.
Knowing when to scale vs. iterateMaking the call to expand ahead of full readiness kept us from losing the market window. It was uncomfortable, but right.
What I'd do differently
Invest in therapist onboarding earlierAdoption often stalled because therapists weren't confident prescribing it — not because patients disliked the product.
Prioritize EHR integration from the startInteroperability became a blocker for larger hospital networks and delayed enterprise expansion by 12–18 months.
Build a direct patient feedback loopFor the first few years, patient feedback came through clinical intermediaries — a filtered signal that made us slow to catch usability issues.
Document tradeoff decisions more rigorouslyDecisions made in year two were often re-litigated in year five with no record of why they were made.
