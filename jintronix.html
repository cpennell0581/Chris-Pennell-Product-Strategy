00 · Background and Product Context

Seven years building at the intersection of behavior, data, and delivery

I'm Chris Pennell, a Product Manager and UX Lead with 10+ years delivering consumer-facing digital products across healthcare, wellness, and enterprise software. I'm completing my MBA at Rice University's Jones Graduate School of Business with a focus on product strategy and digital innovation.

Jintronix was my deepest build: seven years as the founding PM, going from a blank Miro board to clinical deployment across North America. It required competitive analysis to define the market opportunity, continuous usability research to shape the product, and agile delivery leadership to scale it, all at the same time, with clinical outcomes as a hard constraint on every decision.

My role at Jintronix

I was the first and only PM for the first three years. That meant owning competitive analysis, usability research, roadmap, design direction, stakeholder communication, and delivery, with no PM infrastructure to inherit.

As the company scaled I built and led a cross-functional team of designers, engineers, and clinical advisors. I ran agile ceremonies and made sure research outputs moved into engineering work without delay every sprint.

The north star: get a patient to finish their prescribed program. Every product decision was evaluated against that.

The company context

Jintronix was a HealthTech startup built on a hypothesis that competitive analysis confirmed: no digital rehab platform had solved patient engagement. The market was full of scheduling tools and exercise libraries. The engagement gap was the opening.

When I joined, there was a prototype and a hypothesis. When I left, there were 500+ clinical clients, peer-reviewed outcome data, and a product that therapists recommended by name.

We had to figure out both the product and the market simultaneously, with clinical credibility as the primary adoption gate at every stage.

01 · Competitive Analysis and Problem Definition

A competitive analysis confirmed the market gap and shaped the entire product thesis

Before writing a user story, I ran a competitive analysis of the digital rehabilitation technology landscape, benchmarking platforms on patient engagement mechanics, clinical workflow integration, outcome measurement, and provider adoption models. The picture was consistent: the space was scheduling tools, exercise libraries, and telehealth extensions. Nobody had built the engagement layer.

I presented those findings to Jintronix's leadership and clinical advisors with a clear recommendation: the opportunity was not another exercise library. It was building the engagement layer the entire category was missing, with enough clinical credibility to earn therapist prescription. That recommendation became the product thesis and shaped the roadmap for the next three years.

No competitor had solved engagement, the category's defining problem
Competitive analysis showed 50 to 65% of patients don't complete home exercise programs. Every existing digital platform treated this as a given. None had built the feedback loop, progress visibility, or behavioral mechanics needed to change it.
Therapists were making clinical decisions without adherence data
Clinicians had no visibility into what patients did between appointments. They were managing recovery based on self-report, a gap that no competitive platform had addressed and that represented both a clinical risk and a clear provider pain point.
The engagement gap was the opening, confirmed by competitive benchmarking
No platform in the space competed on patient engagement. The question was whether gamification mechanics could be deployed with enough clinical rigor to earn therapist trust at the enterprise sales stage.

The competitive analysis didn't just confirm the problem. It defined the strategy. We weren't entering a crowded market. We were creating a category.

02 · Usability Research and Customer Insights

Ethnographic research inside rehab centers found things surveys would have missed

Before any design work started, I planned and conducted ethnographic research across multiple rehabilitation centers, observing real patient behavior and therapist workflows in actual clinical sessions. Not clipboard-and-survey research. Contextual inquiry: watching where patients disengaged, how therapists coached, and where the experience broke down under real conditions.

I synthesized those behavioral observations into a structured findings report and presented recommendations to Jintronix's clinical advisors and leadership. Each finding was translated into a testable hypothesis before any design or engineering investment was made. The research established three north-star KPIs: adherence rate, average session length, and patient-reported satisfaction. Those governed every subsequent product decision.

Research methodology mapping the behavioral gap between prescription and completion
Finding and Recommendation
Real-time feedback was the missing variable. Patients who got immediate feedback, even just an audio cue for correct form, stayed engaged much longer. I recommended building real-time motion sensing feedback as the core MVP mechanic, not a phase 2 enhancement.
Finding and Recommendation
Progress visibility drove return behavior. Patients who could see improvement over time, session counts, range-of-motion gains, comparison to their own baseline, were far more likely to come back. I recommended personal progress curves as the primary gamification layer.
Finding and Recommendation
Therapist trust was the adoption gate, not patient preference. Patients used what their therapist recommended. Any go-to-market approach that didn't win the therapist first wasn't going to scale. I presented this to leadership with a recommendation to co-develop the therapist dashboard as an equal MVP priority.
Finding and Recommendation
Gamification had to be clinically invisible. The word made clinicians uncomfortable. User testing with therapists confirmed that game mechanics embedded inside a clinical-grade interface performed better than consumer-facing game features presented as-is.

03 · Key Recommendations and Product Decisions

Three recommendations I made to leadership, and the analysis behind each

Building a 0-to-1 product in a regulated clinical market means making recommendations with incomplete information, competing stakeholder priorities, and real delivery pressure. Each of the following was grounded in research and competitive data, and each materially shaped the product's eventual market position.

Recommendation 1 Build the therapist dashboard as an equal MVP priority, not a phase 2 feature
The analysis

Usability research confirmed that patients used what their therapist recommended. Competitive analysis showed no existing platform had invested in therapist-facing tooling. The adoption gateway was the prescriber, not the patient.

My recommendation

I presented an analysis to engineering and leadership recommending co-development of the therapist dashboard alongside the patient experience, despite the scope increase. I framed the recommendation around go-to-market risk, not development cost.

The outcome

The therapist dashboard became our most powerful enterprise sales tool. Clinicians with real-time adherence and outcome data became product advocates. Those advocates drove the referral network that took us to 500+ clinical clients without a paid acquisition model.

The insight: In clinical markets, the prescriber is the first customer. Winning the patient without winning the therapist is a channel that doesn't scale.
Recommendation 2 Cut social comparison features despite strong engagement data
The analysis

Pilot data showed leaderboards and peer benchmarking drove higher engagement. But clinical advisor review flagged that comparing recovery rates across patients with different diagnoses was clinically inappropriate and a liability in enterprise sales conversations.

My recommendation

Cut social comparison from the MVP. Redirect gamification to self-referential progress: patients competed only against their own prior performance. I presented the enterprise sales risk alongside the engagement data so leadership could weigh both.

The outcome

Short-term engagement metrics dipped slightly. Long-term, the decision unlocked hospital and senior care facility partnerships that would have been impossible with social comparison in the product. The clinical credibility premium far outweighed the engagement delta.

The insight: In regulated markets, features that patients love but clinicians can't defend become enterprise sales liabilities regardless of engagement metrics.
Recommendation 3 Scale ahead of full readiness. The market window was closing.
The analysis

After year two, the product had strong pilot data but known rough edges. The clinical team wanted more iteration. Sales had three hospital networks ready to sign. Competitive analysis showed new entrants beginning to surface. Waiting meant losing first-mover position in a market we had defined.

My recommendation

I recommended scaling, presenting a risk analysis to leadership that distinguished cosmetic limitations from functional ones. I proposed expanding with existing partners while addressing known gaps in concurrent sprints. The limitations were manageable. The market window was not.

The outcome

All three hospital networks signed within the quarter. The cosmetic limitations the clinical team had raised were addressed in two sprints. The first-mover position we protected became a meaningful barrier to entry for later entrants.

The insight: "Good enough to ship" and "good enough to scale" are different thresholds. In a nascent market, speed to scale is a strategic asset.

From prototype to standard of care, seven years of hypothesis-driven delivery

Every sprint followed the same framework: define a testable hypothesis, build the minimum viable version, measure against north-star KPIs, and present findings with a recommendation for the next cycle. I ran agile ceremonies across a cross-functional team of designers, engineers, and clinical advisors, making sure research outputs moved into the backlog without delay and no team was waiting on product decisions.

06 · Retrospect

Seven years of lessons in evidence-based digital product leadership

This was the engagement that shaped how I approach product. The decisions were real, the stakes were clinical, the competitive landscape was evolving, and the tradeoffs had measurable consequences. The habit of grounding every recommendation in research and competitive data, and presenting it to leadership in business terms, started here.

What went well
Competitive analysis before any product investment Starting with a structured benchmark of the digital rehab landscape gave us a defensible product thesis before we spent a dollar on design or engineering. It also gave leadership a business case, not just a user need.
Ethnographic research before structured methods Spending weeks inside real rehab centers gave us behavioral insights that survey research would have completely missed. The core product mechanics came directly from observing where patients disengaged in real sessions.
Treating the therapist as the first customer The recommendation to build therapist tooling as an MVP priority shaped go-to-market, enterprise sales, and clinical credibility for the next five years. It was the single most important call I made.
Hypothesis-driven agile delivery throughout Every sprint was organized around a testable assumption. That kept the backlog honest, gave leadership clear evidence for roadmap decisions, and prevented the feature accumulation that kills early-stage products.
What I'd do differently
Build a structured clinician onboarding program earlier Adoption stalled repeatedly not because patients disliked the product, but because therapists weren't confident prescribing it. A formal onboarding program in year one would have accelerated enterprise deployment.
Prioritize EHR integration from the start Interoperability with electronic health record systems became an enterprise sales blocker for larger hospital networks and delayed expansion by 12 to 18 months. The competitive analysis flagged this risk early. I should have pushed harder to get it on the year-two roadmap.
Build a direct patient usability feedback loop earlier For the first few years, patient feedback came through clinical intermediaries. Therapists reported what they heard. That filtered signal meant we were slow to catch usability issues patients experienced directly.
Document product decisions more rigorously as the team scaled Decisions made in year two were relitigated in year five with no documented rationale. A living decision log, grounded in the research and competitive analysis that drove each call, would have saved significant rework.
