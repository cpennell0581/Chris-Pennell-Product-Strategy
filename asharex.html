Making fine art investment feel accessible

aShareX set out to let everyday investors buy fractional shares in museum-quality art. I led research, backlog strategy, and delivery across four live pilot auctions, then pushed through the product decision that mattered most: optional auto-bidding for fractional bidders.

01 · The problem

The market was exclusive. The product risk was emotional.

The mechanics were novel, but the deeper barrier was trust. Users were being asked to make real-money decisions in a category they barely understood, under live-auction pressure, without a familiar mental model to lean on.

  • Traditional fine art investing was effectively closed to anyone without insider access and large amounts of capital.
  • Fractional bidding sounded exciting in theory, but first-time users worried about making mistakes in front of a moving market.
  • Fast pacing multiplied the anxiety. Fractional bidders had to track bids, lots, and remaining allocation in real time.
Working hypothesis

If we make the auction feel guided, not intimidating, first-time investors will bid once and return for the next lot.

Pre-auction listing views did more than show the asset. They had to create enough confidence for a user to believe they belonged in the room.

What we got wrong at first

We assumed better mechanics would naturally create adoption. The research said otherwise: education and emotional comfort were the real unlocks.

Product frame

This was less “build an auction interface” and more “design a trust ramp into a new financial behavior.”

02 · Research loop

Four pilots. One disciplined learning system.

I ran the engagement as a repeating loop: live auction, post-session interviews, synthesis, prioritization, sprint planning, ship, repeat. Nothing hit the backlog without a research rationale attached.

Research

Post-auction interviews with both full and fractional bidders after each pilot.

Product

Synthesis into prioritized recommendations for the founder and engineering lead.

Delivery

Sprint planning, backlog grooming, standups, and retros across the InfoBeans team.

  • 63% of fractional bidders wanted a pre-auction simulation before risking real money.
  • 85% wanted less manual pressure once the auction started moving fast.
  • The strongest adoption drivers were trust, clarity, and confidence, not feature novelty.
What shipped from the research

Test Drive an Auction emerged directly from user requests for practice. It gave people a chance to learn the rhythm before participating live.

03 · The hardest call

The founder wanted manual bidding. The users wanted relief.

This was the make-or-break recommendation. The CEO preferred a purist manual-bidding experience. Pilot data showed that fractional bidders were getting overwhelmed and disengaging.

Wanted less manual pressure

Recommendation

Add optional auto-bidding for fractional bidders, not as a replacement for manual control, but as a confidence scaffold.

Why it worked

I framed the feature as expanded choice rather than a philosophical shift. Manual bidding stayed. Auto-bidding simply protected newer investors from pace-driven drop-off.

Leadership lesson

Research is most valuable when it can survive disagreement with a founder’s instinct. This was one of those moments.

05 · Reflection

What this taught me about 0-to-1 product leadership

  • Novel products need behavior design as much as interface design. If users do not feel safe, novelty reads as risk.
  • Metrics matter more when they are defined before the first pilot. Otherwise, every finding becomes a post-hoc argument.
  • Founder alignment gets easier when the recommendation is grounded in clear evidence and framed around user choice.
What I would do earlier

Instrument analytics from pilot one. Some of the most useful behavioral questions were only measurable later in the program.

What I would do faster

Ship auto-bidding sooner. The signs were there before we formally made the call.

Why this case belongs in the portfolio

This is the project where product judgment meant protecting the user from a system they technically could have used, but would not have trusted long enough to love.

That is the version of product leadership I want to show: research-driven, commercially literate, and willing to challenge preference when the evidence points somewhere better.
